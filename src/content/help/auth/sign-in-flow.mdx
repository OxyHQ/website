Signing in to Oxy looks deceptively simple — type your username, type your password, you're in. Here's what's actually happening behind the curtain.

## The steps

<Steps>
  <Step title="Username lookup">
    When you type your username, Oxy fetches a tiny public profile (color, avatar, display name). This is so the next screen can be branded for you, which is also a phishing defense: a fake login screen can't fake your colors.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Password (or passkey)">
    You enter your password — never sent in cleartext, hashed client-side before transmission. If you have a passkey set up, you can skip the password entirely.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Two-factor">
    If you've enabled 2FA, you'll enter the rotating code from your authenticator or approve a push notification.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Session provisioning">
    The server hands back two tokens: a short-lived access token (1 hour) and a long-lived refresh token (30 days). Both are tied to this specific device.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Identity restore">
    On first sign-in, your private key is loaded from secure device storage — encrypted with a key derived from your recovery phrase. If this is a brand-new device, you'll be asked to restore from your recovery phrase.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Why so many steps?

Most are invisible — they happen in milliseconds. The point is that no single compromise (a password leak, a stolen device, a server breach) gives an attacker your full identity.
